# Feedback Framework

A research-based framework for providing effective, growth-oriented feedback to students.

## The 3-Part Feedback Model

### 1. Praise (What Went Well)

**Characteristics of Effective Praise**
- Specific and concrete
- Links to learning objectives
- Focuses on effort and strategy
- Authentic and genuine

**Bad**: "Good job!"
**Good**: "I notice you used the quadratic formula correctly and showed all your steps. Your organization made it easy to follow your thinking."

### 2. Next Steps (What to Improve)

**Characteristics of Effective Next Steps**
- Specific and actionable
- Focused on the work, not the student
- Provides a clear path forward
- Appropriate to student level

**Bad**: "Needs improvement on writing."
**Good**: "Try adding topic sentences at the beginning of each paragraph to help readers understand your main idea."

### 3. Question (For Further Thinking)

**Purpose**
- Encourages reflection
- Promotes metacognition
- Opens dialogue
- Guides future learning

**Examples**
- "What strategy did you use to solve this problem?"
- "How might you apply this to a different situation?"
- "What would you do differently next time?"

## Feedback by Subject Area

### Math Feedback

**Focus Areas**
- Problem understanding
- Strategy selection
- Calculation accuracy
- Explanation clarity
- Efficiency

**Template**
"Your [specific strength]. To grow, consider [specific improvement]. Have you thought about [question]?"

### Writing Feedback

**Focus Areas**
- Thesis/clarity
- Organization
- Evidence/analysis
- Conventions
- Style/voice

**Template**
"I notice you [strength]. Consider [improvement] to strengthen your writing. What if you [question]?"

### Science Feedback

**Focus Areas**
- Question quality
- Experimental design
- Data interpretation
- Conclusion reasoning
- Lab procedure

**Template**
"Your [specific strength] shows strong scientific thinking. To deepen your analysis, consider [improvement]. How might [question] change your conclusions?"

### History/Social Studies Feedback

**Focus Areas**
- Thesis/argument
- Evidence use
- Historical context
- Multiple perspectives
- Causality

**Template**
"Your [strength] demonstrates good historical thinking. Consider [improvement] to strengthen your argument. What evidence would support [question]?"

## Feedback Timing

| Timing | Purpose | When to Use |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **Immediate** | Quick checks, redirect thinking | During practice |
| **Same Day** | Timely guidance | Short assignments |
| **24-48 Hours** | Thoughtful feedback | Medium assignments |
| **Weekly** | Pattern recognition | Ongoing work |
| **Post-Assessment** | Summarize learning | Tests/projects |

## Feedback Delivery Methods

### Written Feedback
- Use rubrics with comments
- Marginal notes on drafts
- Conference notes
- Digital annotation

### Verbal Feedback
- Conferences (in-person or virtual)
- Small group instruction
- Whole class feedback
- Peer feedback facilitation

### Audio/Video Feedback
- Personal connection
- Nuance and tone
- Modeling thinking
- Time efficiency

## The Feedback Sandwich

**Structure**
1. Positive observation
2. Constructive criticism
3. Positive closing

**Use When**
- Student is sensitive
- Major revision needed
- Building relationship

**Caution**: Don't overuse - can dilute feedback quality

## Student Response to Feedback

### Encouraging Action
- "Your feedback helped me understand..."
- "I'll try [specific strategy] next time..."
- "Can you help me understand [concept]?"

### Follow-Up Strategies
- Revision opportunities
- Conference requests
- Peer feedback cycles
- Self-assessment reflection

## Teacher Self-Feedback

**Reflect on Feedback Practices**
- What feedback was most effective?
- Which students responded to feedback?
- What feedback did I not provide?
- How can I make feedback more efficient?

**Improvement Strategies**
- Use feedback templates
- Focus on 2-3 key criteria
- Incorporate peer feedback
- Use digital tools for efficiency
